## Body sections

### Data engineering: automating the pipeline
Manually mapping a source database onto a standard model used to take
specialist teams weeks. Automated ETL pipelines now handle much of this
work, making the process faster and more consistent — and freeing skilled
people to focus on judgment calls rather than repetitive mapping.

### NLP: unlocking unstructured data
A huge amount of clinically meaningful detail lives in free-text notes
(Module 6). Natural language processing can extract structured information
— like smoking status, symptom mentions, or treatment response — from that
text, turning previously unusable data into something analysts can work
with directly.

### ML phenotyping
Rather than relying on a single diagnosis code (which can be missing or
wrong), machine learning models can combine many signals — codes, labs,
medication history, notes — to more accurately identify which patients
truly have a given condition.

### GenAI: accelerating evidence writing
Generative AI can draft literature summaries, first-pass report sections,
and structured write-ups — compressing work that used to take days into
hours, with a human expert still reviewing and finalizing every output.

### Agentic AI: orchestrating the workflow
Newer "agentic" AI systems can carry out multi-step tasks with less manual
intervention — for example, pulling data, running a standard analysis, and
drafting a summary in one coordinated flow, with a person checking the
result rather than running each step by hand.

### The business value, in plain terms
Faster time-to-evidence. Lower cost per study. More consistent quality
across teams and projects. And the ability to scale to far larger, more
diverse datasets than manual methods ever could. This module deliberately
focuses on *why it matters to the business*, not the technical
implementation — that's a deeper topic for another day.
